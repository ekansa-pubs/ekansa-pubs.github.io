Make a Fool of Me, Please

Eric C. Kansa



This represents a first attempt to make sense of what is happening, from the perspective of a digital archaeologist and open access advocate. Beware! It is my first pass at trying to come to grips with a new reality. It will be somewhat rambling as I haven’t fully integrated my thoughts into anything close to coherence.

Erasing digital history is a big-deal for those of us who want to safeguard our society’s digital and cultural memory. We already found it very hard in a society largely indifferent to our work. Now we have to do it in a situation fast becoming overtly hostile. I’ll get to some of those implications later, especially all the institutional failures that got us here, but first I want to talk about the immediate emergency.

Comforting Fascism

There's no getting away from this. There's been some talk of how "coastal elites" need to better understand the people and problems of "fly-over" country. I'm sure that's the case, as a humanist / social scientist I'm in the business of "better understanding" after all. We have friends, family, and colleagues in fly-over country and our work often takes us all over the US. We have some friends and family that have gone through cycles of unemployment and we also have some friends and family in fly-over country that enjoy higher standards of living, with much bigger homes, boats, vacation time, etc. While we all have stresses, in my small social network of "Red America", I rarely see the kinds of working hours, the stresses and precarity of uncertain and cut-throat grant funding, and relentless performance pressures that we routinely face working at the margins of a broken academic system. And yet, many of them voted for Trump, even though I bet my personal economic anxiety matches or exceeds theirs. So why?

Most people, including my loved ones who voted for Trump, like their comfort zone. They are not evil, they do not want to hurt anyone, they just don't want their comfort zone disrupted. Sadly, that desire to maintain their comfort zone, and go about their lives includes a large dose of willful denial of reality. It is hard to see and grasp structural racism or police brutality. It's a lot easier to dismiss these issues as "whining". It is hard to acknowledge the reality of climate change and the hard questions it raises about so much of the world order. Similarly, "Hollywood" and "the Liberal Media" which all occasionally manage to raise questions about gender, race and other issues can be resented for intruding into the comfort zone. And finally, there is immigration. Immigration threatens one's comfort zone, since it brings one face to face with difference and the unknown.

In the research community, we also try not to break our comfort zones. We can also lack much self-awareness of our own ethical blind-spots. As I've argued for years, many academic institutions, typically associated with the Left, can be terribly dehumanizing and exploitative. But a worse sin is insularity. We tend to talk among ourselves (our comfort zone), often avoiding confrontation, even concerns central to our work. You know those awkward moments with family when topics like religion and politics come up? Well in the interest of being polite and not wanting to cause offense, I personally largely kept my mouth shut and talked about food and weather (but not climate, because you know, touchy). Don't we share just a little complicit in the rise of White Nationalism (aka Nazism) that is now taking control of the US Republic?

Now What?

I invested my heart and soul in this past election, first supporting Bernie Sanders out of great enthusiasm (and money), and then Hillary Clinton out of great fear (and more money). I really, really tried because I was so worried about waking up exactly where we are now. Anyone who followed me on Twitter will remember my periodic panic attacks starting in July 2016 when I realized Trump could win. They will also remember my worries about the polls and the "Black Swan" possibilities that these polls missed critical aspects of reality in their data models. After all, I’m really interested in social science data from a "method and theory" perspective, and I'm very skeptical of "data = truth" claims. Nevertheless, pretty much everyone told me to relax.

Why does Cassandra (you know, this Cassandra) always have to be right about the worst of all outcomes?

My worst fears are now coming true, and I’m learning to trust myself more. The first thing to remember is to get yourself out of the habit of thinking that anything is normal, and that any norm, or law, or constitutional protection will be respected.
